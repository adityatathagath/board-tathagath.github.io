Friday Commentary

US–India Trade Deal: Tariff Cuts and Changes in Key Fact Sheet
“Starting with the big trade story of the week: the US–India interim trade deal announced on 6 February. The headline is a massive tariff reduction—down to 18% from 50%—unlocking a US$500 billion purchase framework over five years. But what’s interesting is how the White House fact sheet has evolved in the last 72 hours.
Initially, the language suggested India was ‘committed to purchase’ US$500 billion of US goods and services, but after pushback from New Delhi, that’s been softened to ‘intends to buy,’ giving India much more flexibility and reducing the risk of rigid fiscal commitments.
On the agricultural side, pulses have been quietly removed from the tariff‑cut examples in the updated fact sheet—so India retains more negotiating room on sensitive food items. And finally, references to India ‘ending’ its digital services tax and dismantling ‘discriminatory measures’ have been diluted or dropped entirely.
In short, the deal is still a material win—it improves market access, supports India’s export sectors like textiles, pharma, and tech, and has already lifted sovereign credit commentary to ‘stable.’ But the revised language shows this is a balanced framework, not a one‑sided concession, and it leaves India with policy flexibility on agriculture and digital taxation.”


US Data Releases
“Moving to the US data cluster that dropped in the last hour: we got the delayed January payrolls at +130,000—well above the consensus of 55k to 70k—and the unemployment rate fell unexpectedly to 4.3% from 4.4% in December. December payrolls were revised slightly down to 48,000 from 51,000, but the headline strength here is clear: the US labour market is still adding jobs at a healthy pace, with health care again leading the gains.
Markets have rallied on this—equities are up, and the broader read is that this supports the Fed’s decision to extend its pause. We’re not seeing a collapsing labour market, so there’s no urgency to restart cuts in the near term.
On the consumer side, December retail sales came in flat at 0.0% month‑on‑month, versus consensus of +0.1% and November’s +0.6%. So we’re seeing a clear slowdown in consumer spending momentum into year‑end, which flags that the economy is on a slower growth trajectory as we move into 2026. The retail sales number keeps the door open for eventual Fed easing, but today’s jobs print means that door stays firmly shut for the next few meetings.”


Central Bank Meeting This Week
“And finally on India: the RBI held its repo rate at 5.25% at the 4–6 February MPC meeting, with a unanimous vote and a neutral stance retained. Governor Malhotra’s message was very clear—‘the economy remains resilient, inflation and growth outlooks are positive, and policy is now in wait‑and‑watch mode’ after 125 basis points of cuts since early 2025.
The focus now is on liquidity management and transmission—the RBI is watching bond‑market hardening closely and may step in with more active operations if term premia rise too sharply. External conditions are described as volatile, but not yet sufficient to derail India’s baseline growth path. And with the trade deal tailwinds now in place, the RBI has even less urgency to ease aggressively in the near term.”


Japan just delivered a historic political outcome, with Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi’s LDP winning 316 of 465 seats and securing a two‑thirds supermajority with its coalition partner. Markets have read this as a strong mandate for tax cuts and higher defence spending, JGB yields have nudged higher and the yen strengthened vs USD as markets priced a slightly higher probability of eventual BoJ normalization alongside stronger domestic growth..

China’s financial regulators have told local banks to limit their US Treasury exposure, asking them to curb new purchases and trim large positions, citing concentration and market volatility risks. Importantly, this guidance excludes China’s official FX reserves, so it’s not a sovereign sell‑off, but it does reinforce the multi‑year trend of China steadily reducing its Treasury holdings—down from a $1.3 trillion peak to around $770 billion today. Markets saw a modest reaction, with 10‑year yields rising 3 to 5 basis points to around 4.25% and the dollar softer on the headlines, but the real significance is structural: as Chinese demand steps back, US debt will need to find new buyers or compensate with higher term premia, adding another layer of geopolitical risk to the Treasury market over the medium term.


In the UK, Prime Minister Keir Starmer has just come through a serious internal revolt over the Peter Mandelson–Epstein scandal, with Scottish Labour openly calling for his resignation, cabinet rallying around him, and Starmer insisting he ‘will never walk away’ from his mandate. Markets have been pricing a political risk premium: gilts underperformed earlier this week, 10‑year yields briefly spiked above 4.6% and the pound and UK equities came under pressure as investors weighed the risk of a leadership change and looser fiscal policy under any successor.
